Abstract

The university course timetabling problem (UCTP) is a well-established NP-hard combinatorial optimization challenge that demands the simultaneous satisfaction of numerous hard and soft constraints involving lecturers, rooms, student groups, and time periods. Traditional manual scheduling approaches are demonstrably inefficient, error-prone, and incapable of scaling to the resource complexity encountered in modern tertiary institutions. This paper presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of UniSched, a web-based automated university timetable scheduling system that employs a custom Genetic Algorithm (GA) to generate conflict-free, optimized weekly class schedules. The system architecture adopts a decoupled client–server model: a Django REST Framework backend exposes RESTful API endpoints for resource management and algorithmic orchestration, while a React Native cross-platform frontend serves both web and mobile clients. The GA engine operates on a pure-Python dataclass representation, leveraging NumPy-vectorized fitness evaluation for a 5–15× speedup over naive Python loops. Adaptive mutation rates, elitism preservation, tournament selection, and single-point crossover collectively drive the search toward high-fitness schedules within configurable generation limits. Empirical results demonstrate that the system consistently produces zero-hard-violation timetables for datasets comprising up to 60 courses, 15 rooms, and 40 weekly timeslots, with typical execution times under 30 seconds. The current Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is designed as an administrator-focused tool with open access—no sign-in or registration is required—allowing scheduling officers to immediately input resources, trigger optimization, visualize the generated grid timetable, and export results in both Excel and CSV formats.

Keywords: University Course Timetabling Problem, Genetic Algorithm, Constraint Satisfaction, NP-Hard Optimization, Adaptive Mutation, Django REST Framework, NumPy Vectorization.

1. Introduction

1.1 Background of Study

The allocation of courses, lecturers, rooms, and student groups across a finite set of weekly time periods constitutes one of the most persistent administrative challenges in tertiary education. This problem, formally classified as the University Course Timetabling Problem (UCTP), belongs to the class of NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems (Even et al., 1976; Cooper & Kingston, 1996). The NP-hard classification implies that no known polynomial-time algorithm guarantees an optimal solution for all instances; as the number of schedulable entities grows, the solution space expands exponentially, rendering exhaustive search computationally infeasible.

Historically, university timetables have been constructed manually by dedicated scheduling officers who rely heavily on domain expertise, spreadsheets, and iterative trial-and-error. While this approach may yield serviceable results for small departments, it is demonstrably inadequate for institutions with hundreds of courses, dozens of lecture halls, and strict regulatory constraints on lecturer workloads, room capacities, and student group conflicts. Manual timetabling is inherently slow, typically consuming weeks of concentrated effort each academic session; it is error-prone, frequently producing double-bookings and capacity violations that surface only after the semester commences; and it is inflexible, requiring near-complete reconstruction when even a single constraint changes mid-term.

1.2 Statement of the Problem

The specific scheduling problem addressed in this work involves assigning each course session to a unique combination of a room and a weekly time period such that: (a) no room is double-booked at any timeslot, (b) no lecturer is scheduled for overlapping sessions, (c) no student group is required to attend two classes simultaneously, and (d) room capacities are not exceeded by enrolled student cohorts. Constraints (a)–(c) are treated as hard constraints whose violation is strictly prohibited, while constraint (d) is modelled as a soft constraint whose violation incurs a graduated penalty.

1.3 Aim and Objectives

The aim of this project is to design and implement an automated, web-accessible timetable scheduling system that replaces manual methods with a computationally intelligent approach grounded in evolutionary computation. The specific objectives are:

  1. To model the UCTP as a constraint satisfaction and optimization problem with clearly delineated hard and soft constraint taxonomies.
  2. To implement a Genetic Algorithm (GA) with adaptive mutation, elitism, and tournament selection as the core optimization engine.
  3. To develop a Django-based RESTful backend that manages institutional resources and orchestrates asynchronous GA execution via a task queue.
  4. To deliver a cross-platform React Native frontend for constraint configuration, scheduling, visualization, and CSV/Excel export.
  5. To evaluate the system's performance in terms of constraint satisfaction, execution time, and user experience.

1.4 Significance of the Study

This study contributes a practical, end-to-end implementation that bridges the gap between the extensive theoretical literature on metaheuristic scheduling (Qu et al., 2009; Lewis, 2008) and the operational needs of Nigerian and Sub-Saharan African universities, where scheduling infrastructure remains predominantly manual.

2. Related Work

2.1 Classical Approaches to University Timetabling

Early computational approaches relied on graph-colouring formulations (Welsh & Powell, 1967). Integer Linear Programming (ILP) has also been applied (Daskalaki et al., 2004), offering mathematical optimality for small instances but impractical for larger ones.

2.2 Metaheuristic Approaches

Simulated Annealing (Abramson, 1991) and Particle Swarm Optimization (Shiau, 2011) have been adapted for timetabling. While effective, each has limitations: SA operates on single trajectories, and PSO is susceptible to premature convergence.

2.3 Genetic Algorithm Approaches and the Proposed Advantage

GAs maintain diverse populations that undergo selection, crossover, and mutation (Goldberg, 1989; Holland, 1992). Previous GA-based systems (Al-Betar et al., 2012; Jha, 2004) lack deployable web interfaces. This paper advances the state of the art with:

  1. Adaptive Mutation Rates (15–30%, fitness-driven)
  2. NumPy-Vectorized Fitness Evaluation (5–15× speedup)
  3. Deployable Web Architecture (RESTful APIs, open-access MVP)

3.5 Technical Stack Justification

3.5.1 Python

Expressive syntax with dataclasses for GA data structures. NumPy provides C-level array operations for the fitness evaluation bottleneck (5–15× speedup via np.unique on structured void views).

3.5.2 Django + DRF

"Batteries-included" framework with ORM, migrations, admin panel. DRF's ModelViewSet maps directly to CRUD operations. AllowAny permissions with automatic anonymous user provisioning for the MVP.

3.5.3 Django-Q2

ORM-backed async task broker. No external dependencies (Redis/RabbitMQ). Frontend polls status at 2-second intervals for real-time progress.

3.5.4 PostgreSQL via Supabase

Managed PostgreSQL with SSL encryption, automatic backups. dj-database-url for environment-specific routing.

3.5.5 React Native + Expo

Single TypeScript codebase for iOS, Android, and web. Expo modules for file system, sharing, and AsyncStorage.

4.6 Sequence Process (Narrative)

Step 1: Administrator opens app. System provisions demo_anonymous user. No login screen.
Step 2: Administrator creates/selects Workspace. ID persisted in AsyncStorage.
Step 3: Administrator creates resources via POST endpoints. Workspace-scoped uniqueness enforced.
Step 4: Administrator adjusts constraint weights. Five predefined logic types with enabled and weight.
Step 5: "🧠 Run Engine" enqueues GA. Backend transforms ORM → dataclasses, generates 40-period grid, returns TaskTracker UUID.
Step 6: Worker runs: (1) 50 random chromosomes, (2) NumPy fitness eval, (3) Tournament selection, (4) Crossover 85%, (5) Mutation 15–30%, (6) Elitism top 3, (7) Cache progress, (8) Terminate at 200 gens or fitness=1.0.
Step 7: Best chromosome serialized into TimetableVersion with fitness, violations, execution time, trend data.
Step 8: Frontend fetches rendered timetable with resolved names, day/period indices, start/end times.
Step 9: Grid renders Mon–Fri, 08:00–18:00. Color-coded blocks positioned by day/hour. Hash function maps course codes to palettes.
Step 10: Export as CSV or Excel. Mobile triggers OS sharing dialog via expo-sharing.

6. Conclusion and Future Work

6.1 Conclusion

This paper presented UniSched, an automated university timetable scheduling system powered by a custom Genetic Algorithm. Key achievements:

  • Zero-Hard-Violation Timetables for datasets up to 60 courses.
  • Sub-30-Second Execution on commodity hardware.
  • Deployable MVP — no installation or authentication required.
  • Export Flexibility — CSV and Excel with professional formatting.

6.2 Future Work

  1. Role-Based Authentication: Re-enable JWT for Lecturer/Student views.
  2. Multi-Objective Optimization: Pareto-optimal solutions.
  3. Drag-and-Drop Overrides: Manual adjustments with conflict validation.
  4. Mobile Push Notifications: FCM integration.
  5. ML Warm-Start: Neural network-generated initial populations.
